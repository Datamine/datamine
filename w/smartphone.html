Smartphone Ownership Review

I held out on smartphones until February 2014. Up until then, I used a regular old dumbphone that I bought at a Walgreens some years back. My primary reason for not wanting a smartphone were mostly that I wasn’t sure whether or not having a smartphone was actually good for you. My friends gave their smartphones ringing endorsements: “I wouldn’t know what to do without it,” “I couldn’t leave my house without my phone,” etc. The flip-side of these endorsements was that they signaled dependency. It seemed to me that the smartphone created its own necessity and then fostered a relationship in which the user would be dependent on the gadget. That seemed unhealthy to me.

I was also intimidated by how smartphones seemed to persistently occupy their users. The endless flurry of notifications is well-known, and it is common to see people in social situations silently staring at their phones in unison. This has been amply discussed and made fun of, but I was actually more concerned with how smartphones eliminate boredom. Smartphones enable the user to be constantly connected to a practically-endless repository of entertainment. If the user chooses to, they may use their phone so as to be fully engaged with media, perpetually entertained, mentally busy, without a moment of pause, ever. I find this concerning, as I believe quite strongly that small periods of ‘boredom’ — time to actually be alone with one’s thoughts and to think — can be creatively very productive. I was afraid of losing those precious moments throughout the day, the few minutes between tasks during which I can just day-dream.

On the other hand, not owning a smartphone came at a considerable price. As a software developer, it effectively excluded me from what is probably the second-most important technological platform, after the internet[0]. On top of that, I obviously wasn’t getting any of the consumer-end benefits of owning a smartphone — concretely, the technological convenience in being constantly connected to the rest of the world. The feeling that the merits of owning a smartphone might outweigh the demerits mounted and gradually nagged me into getting one.

Owning a smartphone has been enormously useful. There are great and obvious benefits to essentially having internet access everywhere I go and carrying an ecosystem of useful services (apps) in my pocket. It has, in short, saved me a great deal of time and money, and has been very helpful in avoiding stressful situations.

However, my fear that owning a smartphone would persistently eliminate boredom and essentially usurp my day-dreaming time has largely become reality. Whenever I find myself with a few seconds to spare, I check my phone — I most often look at Twitter, Hacker News, and Google Finance. The underlying trouble here seems to be that smartphones integrate extremely well with following the news, and that the news are, more or less, a form of mental junk-food. Virtually everything I read on my phone I forget within minutes. It’s usually so irrelevant to my life that it is completely ephemeral, but it feels very engaging and stimulating and good while I’m reading it. Following the news is, with some exception, an unproductive past-time, but it’s quite addictive.

Thus, as feared, I find myself wasting quite a lot of time on my phone. To make things worse, the way in which I am wasting this time is subtly stressful. Smartphones enable constant access to a practically-endless repository of very seductive media. Managing this (and occasionally interacting with this body of media) without letting it consume significant amounts of one’s time and energy is a serious challenge. I think my smartphone has been enormously useful, but I’m also quite aware and concerned that it has replaced those very restorative, highly creatively generative little morsels of time that I used to have to myself with stressful bursts of news-checking.

To conclude: I am rather happy with my smartphone as a useful consumer device. I’m less happy with the relationship I have to my phone. My phone has more control over how I spend my time than I would like it to have. I don’t view this as an issue intrinsic to smartphones, but simply as a challenge for me to manage.

Notes on News

I made several claims about the stressfulness of following the news. I follow news in technology and finance extremely closely due to necessity, and more or less disregard the rest. My reason for this is mostly that I have finite amounts of time and mental energy to expend every day, and I need to allocate those as effectively as possible. Beyond that, I’m not so convinced of the utility of (reading) vast sections of the news. My position can be explained reasonably well by the following articles:

  • Aaron Swartz: I hate the news
  • The Guardian: News is bad for you — and giving up reading it will make you happier
I should note that I think of “news” as a class of media broader than traditional journalism: I consider e.g. tweets, status updates, and most forms of media notifications as news.
